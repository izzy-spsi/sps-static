Why This Matters

Domo Cafe is committed to equal access for all guests. Mishandling a service animal situation can result in serious legal consequences for the café under the Americans with Disabilities Act. This module will give you everything you need to handle any situation correctly.

01 of 09
The Basics
What Is a Service Animal?
Under the ADA, a service animal is a dog (or, in limited cases, a miniature horse) that has been individually trained to perform a specific task for a person with a disability. The task must be directly related to the person's disability.
Examples of Qualifying Tasks

Guiding a person who is blind · Alerting to seizures before they occur · Detecting low blood sugar · Alerting a person who is deaf · Providing balance assistance · Retrieving dropped items · Interrupting PTSD episodes · Reminding a person to take medication

❌ These Do NOT Qualify as Service Animals

Emotional Support Animals (ESAs) · Comfort animals · Therapy animals · Companion pets

The key distinction: the animal must be trained to perform a specific task. Simply providing comfort or emotional support does not qualify under the ADA.

02 of 09
The Most Important Rule
The Only Two Questions You May Ask
If it is not immediately obvious that an animal is a service animal, you may ask only these two questions. No others. Memorize them.
1
"Is the animal required because of a disability?"
2
"What work or task has the animal been trained to perform?"
After You Receive an Answer

Once a guest answers these questions — the conversation ends. You do not need to investigate further, ask follow-up questions, or verify the answer in any way. Accept what they tell you and move on.

03 of 09
Prohibited Questions
What You Are Never Allowed to Ask
These questions are prohibited under the ADA. Asking them can expose Domo Cafe to legal liability. There are no exceptions.
  • "What is your disability?"
  • "Can you show documentation or certification?"
  • "Does the dog have an ID card?"
  • "Can the dog demonstrate its task?"
  • "Why do you need the dog?"
  • "Is that a real service animal?"
Vests, Certifications & Documentation

Service animals are not required to wear a vest, carry ID, or have certification papers. Owners may train their own service animals. No paperwork may be required. If a guest chooses to share documentation — that is their choice, but you may never request it.

04 of 09
Access Rights
Where Service Animals Must Be Allowed
Service animals must be permitted anywhere guests are normally permitted — without exception.
  • All dining areas — indoor and outdoor
  • Waiting areas and ordering lines
  • Outdoor patios open to guests
  • Any area a guest without an animal would be permitted
⚠️ Critical Rules

Do not seat guests with service animals in a separate or isolated area. Do not treat them differently from any other guest. Do not ask them to sit outside or away from other customers. This constitutes discrimination under the ADA.

05 of 09
Rare Situations
When a Service Animal May Be Removed
A service animal may only be asked to leave under two specific conditions. These must be actual, observed behaviors — not assumptions.
Condition 1 — Animal Out of Control

Excessive continuous barking · Aggressive behavior toward guests or staff · Jumping on customers · Running freely without handler control

Condition 2 — Animal Not Housebroken

Urinating indoors · Defecating indoors

✓ Required Action on Removal

If a service animal must be removed, the guest must still be offered service. Tell them they are welcome to secure the animal outside and return. You cannot refuse service to the guest.

06 of 09
Practice Scenarios
How to Respond in the Real World
Study these scenarios. These are the situations you are most likely to encounter.
✅ Scenario A — Legitimate Service Dog
Guest
"Yes, my dog alerts me before I have a seizure."
You (Correct Response)
"Thank you. Please let us know if there's anything you need."
The guest identified a task. That's all you need. Seat them normally and move on.
⚠️ Scenario B — Emotional Support Animal
Guest
"This dog helps me feel calm and less anxious."
You (Follow-Up Question)
"What specific task has the dog been trained to perform?"
Guest
"It just... makes me feel better. It doesn't do anything specific."
You (If No Task Identified)
"I understand, and I'm sorry for the inconvenience. Under ADA guidelines, we can only admit animals that are trained to perform a specific task. Emotional support animals aren't covered by the same access rules. You're welcome to dine with us — we'd just need the dog to stay outside."
Always be kind and respectful. Never accusatory. Offer to still serve the guest.
🔴 Scenario C — Disruptive Animal
A service animal is barking continuously and lunging toward other guests.
You
"I'm sorry to interrupt. Because the animal is not able to be settled right now, we do need to ask that it be taken outside. You are absolutely welcome to continue dining with us — we'd be happy to hold your table."
Remain calm and professional. Get a manager if needed. Always offer continued service to the guest.
07 of 09
Quick Reference
Manager Decision Guide
✓ Allow Entry If

Guest states the animal is required because of a disability AND guest identifies a trained task the animal performs. That's it. Both conditions = allow.

✗ Never Require

Certification · Documentation · Vest or identifying gear · ID card · Demonstration of the task

✗ Remove Only If (Observed, Not Assumed)

Animal is out of control (barking, aggression, running free) OR animal is not housebroken (elimination indoors). Guest must still be offered service.

Food & Care

Domo Cafe is not responsible for feeding, watering, walking, or supervising service animals. The handler is responsible. If a guest requests a plain food item for their animal and the café can reasonably provide it, treat it like any normal request.

Knowledge Check
Quick Quiz
Answer all 5 questions. You need 4/5 to pass. Select your answer and review the feedback before moving on.
1. A guest enters with a dog. The dog has no vest. What do you do?
✓ Correct! Vests are not required. If it's not obvious the animal is a service animal, ask only the two permitted questions.
✗ Incorrect. Service animals are not required to wear vests or carry documentation. Ask only the two permitted questions.
2. A guest says: "My dog helps me feel calm and comfortable." What do you say next?
✓ Correct! Emotional comfort alone doesn't qualify. Ask the second permitted question to determine if a specific task exists.
✗ Incorrect. Emotional comfort alone doesn't qualify as a service animal task. The correct follow-up is to ask what specific task the animal has been trained to perform.
3. A service animal is barking continuously and guests are complaining. What is the correct action?
✓ Correct! An out-of-control animal may be removed — but the guest must still be offered service.
✗ Incorrect. A disruptive animal may be asked to leave, but the guest must still be offered service. Never remove the guest themselves.
4. Which of the following is a legitimate reason to deny a service animal entry?
✓ Correct! An animal that is not housebroken may be removed. Lack of vest, documentation, or another guest's allergies are not valid reasons.
✗ Incorrect. Only two things justify removal: the animal is out of control, or the animal is not housebroken. None of the other options are valid ADA reasons.
5. After the guest answers your two questions, what should you do?
✓ Correct! Once you receive an acceptable answer, the conversation ends. Seat them normally.
✗ Incorrect. Once a guest provides an acceptable answer, you accept it and treat them like any other guest. No follow-up questions, no isolation, no verification.
